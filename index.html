Alyssa Sierra

Media & Cultural Studies

Growing up, I never knew permanence. My father is a retired US Marine and so that demanded an ever-changing lifestyle, never lingering in one place for too long. Every three years, my family and I were shipped off somewhere new, within the country and out. This lifestyle allowed me to develop skills that many children my age did not possess. Being adaptable was the biggest skill I developed, and I am forever grateful that I learned this skill so young. However, a lasting consequence of the constant moving was a flaw in my character; It’s hard for me to retain the connections I make in the places I live in.

I make friends easily, but when it comes time to leave, I am also quick to leave those memories behind. This includes my extended family, who all have deep roots set in California. When I was about to start high school, my father retired in California so that my brothers and I could connect with our family before it was too late. It was the perfect time to settle down and make lifelong friends like my parents had. I found my people in my junior year of high school and am still friends with them to this day, though it was not too long ago when I first met them. I attend UCR with two of them, and they are some of my closest friends.

I am happy to say that college was not a great challenge for me to adjust to. I credit that to my changing lifestyle and my adaptability. It was not without its challenges, however, but that is only natural for someone transitioning from a high school senior into a college first year. Not only am I furthering my education, but I am also learning how to be an adult, which is difficult and scary. Being in college has made me realize there is not a lot I know about being an adult. The biggest thing that I have come to realize is how expensive it is to live and be a student. Job-searching is also something I realized is difficult and infuriating. As of now, I am still unemployed but continue my job search with eagerness and a hopeful attitude.
